Algebra Explorer

Algebra Explorer is a tool that allows students to freely explore algebra without worrying about making mistakes. Our aim is to give students an intuition for:

  • In 1+2×3 why does 2×3 go first?
  • Why is 1+2−3 different since either 1+2 or 2−3 can go first?
  • Why does 1−2+3 equal (1−2)+3 but not 1−(2+3)?
  • Why does 1+2 equal 2+1 but 1−2 does not equal 2−1?

We do this by visualising the notation via a building blocks representation. For example, 1+2 is:

But the best part of the tool is the interactivity! The rest of this article serves as a tutorial with that in mind, and along the way we hope to reveal some insights you might not have thought about before. Enjoy Algebra Explorer!

Commutativity and Associativity

How can I move around the blocks?

Commutativity and Associativity are quite long and scary words, but in Algebra Explorer they are represented via dragging.

Commutativity Reordering

It may seem obvious that

a+b=b+a

but this is a special trait of + and × that is crucial to algebra. Generally, functions cannot reorder their inputs. Consider the sentence

My friend enjoys maths.

where the verb “enjoys” is a function with “My friend” and “maths” as its inputs. Swapping the order gives:

Maths enjoys my friend.

which doesn’t make any sense at all!

Associativity Grouping

It may also seem obvious that

(a+b)+c= a+(b+c)

but this time consider the sentence

I have a friend with a cat who enjoys maths.

compared to

I have a friend (with a cat) who enjoys maths.

This time the rewritten sentence does make sense (who knows what cats are doing when we’re not looking...) but has a very different meaning.

Inverses

Why can I not reorder − or ÷?

Subtraction

Let’s have a look at what 1−2 looks like in the block representation:

You will notice that there is an entire block with only a minus symbol inside it. This is because subtraction is defined as adding the negation, which is a function with a single input. Reordering the blocks on top of + here results in

Which in standard notation is −2+1. This reveals why we cannot reorder the inputs around a minus: the full notation for 1−2 is 1+(−2) and you must move the entire (−2) to obey the rules of the previous section.

This can be confusing because standard notation removes any + preceding a negation. This is similar to how “do not” is abbreviated into “don’t” in English. Explicitly keeping the − separate also reveals why we cannot add brackets anywhere when there is a minus involved, taking 1−2+3 as an example:

However, grouping the right-hand numbers results in

which is 1+(−2+3), not 1−(2+3). Explicitly showing the minus is useful for explaining the basic rules of algebra, even if the shorthand version is usually more useful for manipulation!

Division

Division is to multiplication what subtraction is to addition: multiplying with the reciprocal, also a function that takes one input. The beauty of the blocks representation is that the exact reordering and grouping rules apply. Let’s see what 2÷3 looks like:

In fact, we could even argue that the ‘complete’ representation of −2+1 from earlier should be 0−2+1 if we wanted to be completely explicit about what is being subtracted from what. These are all examples of pitfalls in our notation that can be confusing, but hopefully seeing and playing with the blocks can help students learn to avoid them.

Notation

Why do we need brackets?

Language

On the surface, languages are simply letters on a page or sounds you make to pass information from one to another. Maths notation (written maths) is exactly the same. They may contain concepts, but you mostly see numbers and other symbols. One of these symbols is the bracket. To understand the bracket, we need to understand what concept it conveys.

BODMAS/BIDMAS/PEMDAS

As we have seen from the examples in the other sections, brackets help us to understand the order of operation. The ones within the brackets are always handled first. If we kept all the brackets, it would look like this:

(((1×2)+(3×4)) -(5÷6))

Almost half of that is written with brackets! Let's also look at using a lot of brackets in English:

The (ordinarily sulky) cat eats (with a grin (on their (rather fluffy) face)).

They are now too hard to read. We need to remove some of them; and that is where BODMAS/BIDMAS/PEMDAS comes in. It is an agreed convention for understanding the order of operations, so that we can cut down on the number of brackets.

OrderNameComments
1stBrackets / Parenthesis Allows for any expression inside of it to go first
2ndOrder / Indices / Exponent Repeated multiplcation
3rdMultiplication / Division Repeated addition (and its inverse)
4thAddition / Subtraction Repeated counting (and its inverse)

Our example above can be fully written without any brackets, because all of the inner brackets have operations that would be done earlier before the outer ones. So those brackets are redundant. The only times we need to use the bracket is if we want to do something earlier that has a later order.

Factorisation

Factorisation is one of those cases where brackets are introduced. Instead of keeping the expression where multiplication goes first, we rewrite it so that addition comes first. Since multiplication usually happens first according to BIDMAS/BODMAS/PEMDAS, we need to insert brackets to tell others that we want addition to happen first.

Click on the + sign, and find the 'Factorise' action. When you factorise, you change the order of the operations, and brackets will appear. To revert your change, either do an undo, or click on the × and find the 'Expand' action.

Exploration of Patterns

What rules are we using?

Algebra

Maths is sometimes called the “Science of Pattern”. The key is to know when a pattern is applicable to the expression. This is where algebra is useful. When we use a letter, we don't need to know exactly what the value of it is, but simply how it can be transformed.

Take factorisation for example, the requirement for factorising is that the two terms being added must share the same factor (are multiplied by the same number). We don't know what that number is, so let's call that a. We can write it as the following pattern:

ab+ac=a(b+c)

Here are some examples that fit the pattern on the left, and some that don't fit:

Matches 2×3+2×5 5×(3+6)+2×5 3×(5+7)+7 ×(5+7)
Mismatches 2×3+4×5 2+3+4 3÷2+2*5

Topics

This project is designed to be as flexible as possible, and apart from the main operations in BODMAS/BIDMAS/PEMDAS, they are all defined by our custom topic files. They specify a list of functions and constants to include, and then a bunch of actions that try to find matches to then transform it when the action is selected.

All files can be found on our Github. You may notice a lot of backslashes, and that's because our plain-text-input relies on that for functions as well as long variable names.

You can also make your custom topics to load into the webpage. You can find it in the menu.
