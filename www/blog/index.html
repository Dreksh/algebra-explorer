Algebra Explorer

Algebra Explorer is a tool that allows students to freely explore algebra without worrying about making mistakes. Our aim is to let give students an intuition for:
  • In 1+2×3 why does 2×3 go first?
  • Why is 1+2−3 different since either 1+2 or 2−3 can go first?
  • Why does 1−2+3 equal (1−2)+3 but not 1−(2+3)?
  • Why does 1+2 equal 2+1 but 1-2 does not equal 2-1?

We do this by visualising the notation via building blocks representation.

For example, 1+2 is represented by this block where 1 and 2 are stacked on top of +, implying that + depends on 1 and 2.

Similarly, 1+2×3 is:

This alternative representation shows more clearly that 2×3 must go first, using levels as an analogy for the dependency relationship between operations, similar to the movements in "Towers of Hanoi" .

This best part of this tool is the interactivity! The rest of this article serves as a tutorial with that in mind, and along the way we hope to reveal some insights you might not have thought about before. Enjoy Algebra Explorer!

Commutativity and Associativity

How can I understand the blocks?

Commutativity and Associativity are quite long and scary words, but in our tool they are represented via dragging.

Commutativity Reordering

It may seem obvious that

a+b=b+a

but this is a special trait of + and × that is crucial to algebra. Generally, functions cannot reorder their inputs. Consider the sentence
My friend enjoys maths.
where the verb is the function, with "My friend" and "maths" as the inputs. Swapping the order gives:
Maths enjoys my friend.
which doesn't make any sense at all!

Associativity Grouping

It may also seem obvious that

(a+b)+c= a+(b+c)

but this time consider the sentence
I have a friend with a cat who enjoys maths.
compared to
I have a friend (with a cat) who enjoys maths.
This time the rewritten sentence does make sense (who knows what cats are doing when we're not looking...) but has a very different meaning.

Inverses

Why do some operations not allow swaps?

Negative Numbers

What does it mean to have -2 cats?

I don't know either.

+(-..) shortened to just -. This of this as abbreviation, similar to "do not" into "don't", "it was" into "'twas"

Let's first define what Addition and Multiplication can mean in simple terms:

Addition: Let's get n more things
Multiplication: Let's make n copies

Inverses can be roughly thought of as "the same operation, but in the other direction". These would be the definitions in simple terms:
' Subtraction: Let's remove n things
Division: Let's split this into n copies

To represent the "the same operation, but in the other direction", it's useful to simply mark the ones that are inverted. This is where the "unary minus" operation comes in, where we simply add - in front of anything, and we understand that it's to be taken away. This is exactly how we got negative numbers, by using this in front of positive numbers.

To represent the unary inverse, or simply 'marking' which inputs go the other direction, we add a block just below the input.

Multiplication, on the other hand, does not have a "unary division". But to illustrate its similarities to Addition and Subtraction, we have opted to represent the block as a unary inverse. The text view of this will add an additional 1 (the multiplicative identity) in front, so it appears like a binary operation (which it is, but not how we're using it).

Notation

Why do we need brackets?

Langauge

On the surface, languages are simply letters on a page or sounds you make to pass information from one to another. Maths notation (written maths) is exactly the same. They may contain concepts, but you mostly see numbers and other symbols. One of these symbols is the bracket. To understand the bracket, we need to understand what concept it conveys.

BODMAS/BIDMAS/PEMDAS

As we have seen from the examples in the other sections, brackets help us to understand the order of operation. The ones within the brackets are always handled first. If we kept all the brackets, it would look like this:

(((1×2)+(3×4)) -(5÷6))

Almost half of that is written with brackets! Let's also look at using a lot of brackets in English:

The (ordinarily sulky) cat eats (with a grin (on their (rather fluffy) face)).

They are now too hard to read. We need to remove some of them; and that is where BODMAS/BIDMAS/PEMDAS comes in. It is an agreed convention for understanding the order of operations, so that we can cut down on the number of brackets.

OrderNameComments
1stBrackets / Parenthesis Allows for any expression inside of it to go first
2ndOrder / Indices / Exponent Repeated multiplcation
3rdMultiplication / Division Repeated addition (and its inverse)
4thAddition / Subtraction Repeated counting (and its inverse)

Our example above can be fully written without any brackets, because all of the inner brackets have operations that would be done earlier before the outer ones. So those brackets are redundant. The only times we need to use the bracket is if we want to do something earlier that has a later order.

Factorisation

Factorisation is one of those cases where brackets are introduced. Instead of keeping the expression where multiplication goes first, we rewrite it so that addition comes first. Since multiplication usually happens first according to BIDMAS/BODMAS/PEMDAS, we need to insert brackets to tell others that we want addition to happen first.

Click on the + sign, and find the 'Factorise' action. When you factorise, you change the order of the operations, and brackets will appear. To revert your change, either do an undo, or click on the × and find the 'Expand' action.
